# Customer Avatars and Customer Journeys

This file defines ProSoccer's four core customer avatars: Carlos (The Fan), Jennifer (The Mom), Tyler (The Athlete), and Mike (The Coach). Each avatar includes demographics, motivations, fears, pain points, customer journey stages, purchasing behavior, and psychographics.

Every agent reads this file at startup. Avatars inform keyword intent classification, content angles, on-page voice, segmented reporting, and competitive positioning. When agents make priority calls or write copy, they should anchor decisions to specific avatars rather than treating the audience as monolithic.

Source: ProSoccer customer research compiled by 7 Rock Marketing, exported from Milanote 2026-04-25.

---

## Avatar 1: Carlos, The Fan

### Demographics

- Age: 25 to 49 (median 33), strong representation from Millennials and Gen X
- Gender: 56% male, 44% female (slight male skew, but highly engaged female fans too)
- Income: Median $85,000; 45% of households over $100k; 50% have discretionary income over $50k
- Location: Global reach, but strongest in US Hispanic communities and urban or suburban centers
- Education: 66% college or post-grad degrees
- Ethnicity: Very diverse, with strong Hispanic representation, also Asian, Middle Eastern, and North African

### Bio

Carlos is 30 years old, living in Atlanta, Georgia. He is part of the wave of newer U.S. soccer fans whose passion explodes every four years for World Cup, but who, lately, watches MLS, NWSL, and Premier League nearly every weekend.

Carlos discovered soccer in college, drawn in by his friend group's mix of backgrounds (Nigerian, Mexican, U.S. military brat) and fell in love with the game's global community.

He splits his loyalty between Atlanta United and Manchester City, with plenty of time for U.S. Women's National Team friendlies.

Carlos's fandom goes far beyond just watching: he arranges viewing parties, posts memes and clips to fan group chats, and was one of the first among his friends to buy a Messi Inter Miami jersey drop.

He is very active on Instagram and YouTube, where he follows both pro players and soccer influencers, and frequently attends live watch parties with other fans.

Carlos's mood rises and falls with game results. He is happiest when sharing the game with friends, some of whom are brand new fans, some "born into it." He likes to collect team gear, invest in limited-edition drops, and even experiments with soccer fashion, knowing style is increasingly tied to soccer culture for Americans under 35.

Carlos and his friends joke about being "plastics" (fair-weather fans), but acquiring authentic merch and posting about matchday rituals gives him real cred in his community.

He is conscious of authenticity (worries about fakes), cost (sometimes split streaming subs), and being part of the growth of the sport in the U.S., especially as women's soccer rises and brands begin to support the fan community.

### Aspirations

Want to be part of something bigger ("join the story" of clubs and community), have access to authentic merch and experiences, mark moments with exclusive or limited-edition drops, pass on passion to family and friends.

### Fears

- Team disappointment
- Purchasing fakes
- Missing out (FOMO)
- Criticism from other fans
- Rising prices limiting access

### Pain Points

- Authenticity concerns (fake products)
- Price sensitivity, high cost of merch and tickets
- Limited availability (special releases)
- Overwhelming options and information
- Finding merch reflecting culture and loyalty

### Customer Journey

- **Awareness:** Social media, team sites, friends; excited, curious, FOMO
- **Consideration:** Compare prices and stores, seek reviews, check authenticity
- **Decision:** Buys if authentic, price is fair, item is available; wants secure checkout and clear delivery info
- **Post-Purchase:** Wears and shows the gear, posts online, may face return, fit, or delivery issues, wants to connect, share, and be rewarded

### Purchasing Behavior

- **Multi-channel:** Online for convenience, but in-person and retail for watch parties and launch events
- **Brand Loyalty:** Strong to team, league, and sponsor brands that "support the sport" (women's fans especially reward sponsors directly)
- **Spend:** Ranges widely ($150 to $500+ a year for the involved segments; more around big tournaments)
- **Triggers:** New league seasons, World Cup or major matches, influencer tie-ins, family or friend gatherings

### Psychographics

- **Personality:** Passionate, loyal, culturally connected, adventurous, curious, influential
- **Values:** Cultural pride, team loyalty, tradition, authenticity, community
- **Interests:** Soccer, sports, gaming, social media, travel, memorabilia
- **Lifestyle:** Social, attends and watches games, sports bar gatherings, family involvement

### Customer Journey Goals by Stage

- **Pre-Purchase (AIDAR: Awareness and Interest):** Capture Carlos during the "Drop Hype" window when limited-edition releases are announced
- **Transaction (AIDAR: Desire and Action):** Maximize the AOV with customization options
- **Post-Purchase (AIDAR: Retention):** Make him the "Cool Guy" at the pub by ensuring his gear feels authentic and exclusive

## Avatar 2: Jennifer, The Mom

### Demographics

- Age: 35 to 50
- Gender: Female (62% of soccer parents)
- Income: $63k average household (wide range)
- Location: Suburban or urban
- Education: College and above, family-oriented
- Family: 1 to 3 children, multiple kids in sports

### Bio

Jennifer is a 41-year-old project manager and mother of two active kids in a Chicago suburb. She juggles full-time work and a family schedule packed with soccer practices, games, and tournaments. Jennifer never played soccer herself, but she has grown into the role of "team mom," the go-to person for snacks, rides, uniform orders, and organizing end-of-season parties. She is in the group chat with other moms, comparing deals and sharing recommended gear, and she regularly consults coaches about what equipment is truly necessary.

Jennifer isn't just a "team mom"; she is the **Chief Logistics Officer**. She feels the crushing pressure of "Pay-to-Play." She resents spending $300 on a uniform kit that arrives late, but she pays it because she fears her child being "left behind." She trusts other moms on Facebook and Reddit more than she trusts brand ads. She isn't looking for "performance" for performance's sake; she is looking for **"Protection"** (from blisters, from heel pain like Sever's disease, and from ACL injuries).

Jennifer researches every purchase, seeking the best combination of price, safety, and durability for her children. She has seen firsthand how quickly her kids outgrow shoes or jerseys, so she plans purchases strategically and looks for deals, without sacrificing on brand trust or quality, especially for safety gear. Jennifer is budget-conscious but will spend extra for peace of mind. Her dream is to see her children develop discipline, make lasting friends, and maybe even win a sports scholarship, but above all, she wants them to have fun and feel included.

### Aspirations

- Support whole child (athletic AND social)
- Scholarships and college opportunities
- Balanced family life, positive memories
- Team connections and friendships

### Fears

- Injury from bad gear
- Overspending or hurting the family budget
- Child losing interest, being excluded, or benched
- Making poor choices (equipment, coaches)
- Toxic parent and sideline drama

### Pain Points (Named Frames)

These are the named pain frames Jennifer expresses in her own words. Agents should use this exact language where it fits.

**"The Growth Spurt Tax"**
Buying $200 cleats in March that don't fit in May. She feels like she is burning money.

**"The Wide Foot Nightmare"**
Most "speed" boots (Nike Mercurial) are too narrow. She dreads the "return loop" of ordering online and having them not fit.

**"Turf Anxiety"**
She reads about ACL tears on Facebook. She doesn't know the difference between FG (Firm Ground), AG (Artificial Grass), and TF (Turf) plates, but she wants the safest one.

**"The Stink"**
The specific bacterial smell of synthetic cleats. She is looking for "hacks" (baking soda, freezer tricks) because normal washing doesn't work.

### Customer Journey

- **Awareness:** Coach or other parents prompt need; child outgrows or wears out gear
- **Consideration:** Researches online, seeks reviews, asks other parents, compares deals, fit, and quality
- **Decision:** Prefers easy, secure checkout, clear return and exchange policies; wants proof of safety and reliability
- **Post-Purchase:** Monitors fit and performance, satisfaction (or returns and exchanges), loyalty if the experience is smooth and the child is happy

### Purchasing Behavior

- High price sensitivity, but pays for quality and safety
- Nike and Adidas for quality, value brands for basics
- $1,000+ per child per year
- Buys seasonally or when gear is needed
- Online for research and deals, in-store for fit

### Psychographics

- **Personality:** Organized, supportive, budget-conscious, invested in child
- **Values:** Child's development, safety, fairness, family, community, balance
- **Interests:** Kids' development and health, parenting, local events
- **Lifestyle:** Busy, manages family schedule, carpooling, tournaments

### Voice of Customer (Direct Quotes)

These quotes come from research interviews. Agents should reference this language when writing copy that addresses Jennifer's concerns.

- "I don't care if they make him run faster, I just want him to stop complaining about his feet hurting."
- "Why does every cool cleat only come in narrow sizes? My kid has wide feet!"
- "The smell of the cleats in the car is unbearable. I need a solution, not just air freshener."
- "I'm terrified of him playing on turf with the wrong shoes. I've heard horror stories about knees."

### Customer Journey Goals by Stage

- **Pre-Purchase:** Find a solution that doesn't waste money or cause injury
- **Transaction:** Make the decision with confidence and zero friction
- **Post-Purchase:** Validate her decision and solve the secondary problems (smell, fit, durability)

## Avatar 3: Tyler, The Athlete or Player

### Demographics

- Age: 6 to 35+ (youth, club, college, adult rec, pro)
- Gender: Increasingly gender-balanced; historic male skew diminishing fast
- Income: Parent-funded (youth); college, rec, and pro pay their own way as they age
- Location: USA (metro and suburban soccer hubs especially), global and international for elite prospects
- Education: In school (youth, high school, college); varied for adults
- Level: Ranges from recreational, club or select, high school varsity, college or NCAA, all the way up to trial or pro

### Bio

Tyler is a 17-year-old high school junior in Dallas, Texas, where soccer is a way of life for him. He has been playing since age six, started at local rec, and progressed to the city's top select club, earning a spot on the varsity squad as a sophomore. Tyler rises before school for extra training: he'll juggle in the garage, complete skills drills from YouTube, or text clips of his best moves to teammates and his coach.

Obsessed with improvement, Tyler studies pro matches and follows his favorite players, and what gear they use, on social media. His room is plastered with posters of his idols, his shelves lined with worn-out cleats he can't bring himself to throw away. Gear matters to Tyler: the right cleats, compression gear, or tech socks feel like a piece of his identity, and he'll work summer jobs or swap with teammates to afford the latest drop from Nike or Adidas.

Tyler is motivated by competition. He thrives on his coach's praise, his parents' encouragement, and (most of all) outperforming his rivals. But with ambition comes anxiety: he frets over missing tryouts, losing his edge, or injuries that would keep him sidelined. He is tough on himself but also the first to text encouragement to a struggling teammate. Tyler dreams of a college scholarship, playing at the next level, and building lifelong friendships through the game.

**Key traits:** Driven, optimistic, self-critical, tech-savvy, thrives on recognition, quick to adapt

**Fears and motivations:** Fears falling behind, letting others down, or missing a big opportunity; driven by achievement, peer respect, and love of the game.

### Aspirations

- Level up their game: making varsity, club select, college team, or going pro
- Earn respect and recognition from coaches, parents, and peers
- Win tournaments, earn scholarships, play at higher levels
- Personal bests: more goals, more assists, personal records
- Build lasting friendships and become a "known" player in their soccer community

### Fears

- Not making team, not getting play time, or losing to a rival
- Suffering an injury that halts progress
- Gear failure at crucial moments (e.g., cleat blows out in tryout or final)
- Disappointing parents, coaches, or letting down the team
- Cost barriers preventing ongoing play or upgrades

### Pain Points

- Finding gear that truly fits (size and comfort) and really enhances performance, not just hype
- Pressure to improve and keep up with competition (internal, parental, peer)
- Balancing cost and "must have" status of new cleats, tech, and accessories
- Risk of injury; worries about equipment letdowns
- Information overload: which pro, brand, or coach advice is actually trustworthy?
- FOMO on latest brand drops or team-only gear

### Customer Journey

- **Awareness:** Notices pro, peer, or trainer using new gear, feels their gear is holding them back, wants an "edge"
- **Consideration:** Watches reviews, listens to teammates, tries on or demos at stores, analyzes performance impact
- **Decision:** Prioritizes fit, comfort, and peer or pro endorsement; wants risk-free purchase (easy returns and exchanges)
- **Post-Purchase:** Uses in training and games, assesses impact (psychological and physical), shares experience on social media or group chat, expects recognition, already planning next upgrade

### Purchasing Behavior

- **Price sensitivity:** Varies. Youth buy what parents approve; serious club or adult players will save and spend for performance.
- **Brands:** Nike, Adidas, Puma; heavily influenced by professional endorsements and what their soccer idols and peers wear
- **Frequency:** Cleats 1 to 2 times per year; apparel, balls, and training gear seasonally or as needed; may impulse-buy for drop releases or pro tie-ins
- **Spend:** $300 to $1,000+ annually
- **Where:** Specialty soccer shops, trusted online retailers; recommendations are key

### Psychographics

- **Personality:** Highly competitive, ambitious, disciplined, self-motivated, team-focused, craves improvement, resilient under pressure
- **Values:** Excellence, fair play, achievement, teamwork, continuous growth, status, loyalty to team and coach
- **Interests:** Skills training, new gear and tech, watching pro soccer, fitness and nutrition, e-sports, social media content (soccer memes, tutorials, skills challenges)
- **Lifestyle:** Rigid practice and match schedule, balance school, work, and travel; frequent peer, coach, and parent feedback loop; social circle often defined by teammates

### Customer Journey Goals by Stage

- **Pre-Purchase (AIDAR: Awareness and Interest):** Capture him when the "Hype" is highest or the need is urgent
- **Transaction (AIDAR: Desire and Action):** Be the reliable source when others are sold out and increase AOV with "Pro" add-ons
- **Post-Purchase (AIDAR: Retention):** Make him look cool and play well (the "Flex")

## Avatar 4: Mike, The Coach

### Demographics

- Age: 32 to 54
- Gender: Male
- Income: Varies, ranging from part-time volunteer ($30k) to paid club or academy coach ($100k+ at highest levels)
- Location: Local to the teams he coaches, mostly urban or suburban, but can travel for tournaments
- Education: Mix of backgrounds; many with "D" or "C" USSF licenses, former players with some college experience
- Experience: 6 to 25+ years coaching (can include coaching his own children's teams or working up through rec to club or academy)

### Bio

Mike is a 43-year-old high school science teacher and head boys varsity soccer coach in suburban Ohio. Soccer has always been his passion; he played through college and began coaching his oldest son's recreational team over a decade ago. Mike's competitive streak once drove him as a player, but now it channels into building successful, disciplined teams where every boy feels valued and improves, on and off the field.

A stickler for structure, Mike runs practices with military precision yet also leads with humor and approachability. He knows half his boys will likely never play beyond high school, so he mixes rigorous drills with mentorship, teaching life lessons about teamwork, effort, and failing forward. He meticulously manages the team's limited budget, squeezing every bit of value from club dues to buy balls, cones, and fresh goal nets, always seeking durability for the money.

Mike finds fulfillment in seeing quiet kids develop confidence and watching his teams come together as a unit. He faces challenges (parents who second-guess tactics, city field conditions, and ever-changing safety guidelines) but is respected for putting players first. He networks with other coaches, studies professional games, and even hosts Zoom "chalk talks" during off-season. Beyond wins, Mike measures success by how many young men return to thank him for helping them grow.

**Key traits:** Disciplined, mentor, leader by example, practical about budgets, compassionate motivator

**Fears and motivations:** Fears player injury, parent blowback, or failing to deliver a great experience; driven by making boys better players and people, and earning the respect of players, parents, and peers.

### Aspirations

- Build a team that plays "the right way" and supports each other
- Win tournaments while improving player skills and confidence
- Advance own coaching education to higher licenses or even paid club or academy work
- Become a mentor and role model for youth players
- Earn respect among peers, parents, and within the soccer community

### Fears

- Player injuries caused by poor gear or unsafe fields
- Conflict with dissatisfied parents
- Equipment malfunctions during key moments
- Losing top players to other teams or clubs
- Not meeting development or competitive goals (team or individual)
- Damaging reputation among parents and peers

### Pain Points

- Tight budgets, must maximize value for team or club
- Equipment needing to last multiple seasons
- Parents pressuring about playing time and tactics
- Disparity in player skill and motivation
- Transporting and storing equipment
- Keeping up with coaching trends and certifications

### Customer Journey

- **Awareness:** Needs to equip new season or team (prompted by equipment checks, new players, or lost gear)
- **Consideration:** Compares bulk pricing, durability (brand experiences and feedback from other coaches and groups), consults coaching organizations and forums
- **Decision:** Prioritizes reliability, price, easy bulk ordering and invoicing, and confirmed on-time delivery
- **Post-Purchase:** Uses gear, evaluates performance and replacement needs, expects responsive support from suppliers on issues

### Purchasing Behavior

- Very high price sensitivity, especially in youth and amateur clubs; spends more for must-have durability
- Prefers brands known for reliability (Kwik Goal for goals, Nike or Adidas for balls, general value for cones and pinnies)
- Spends $500 to $2,000+ per year for all team equipment needs, mostly before season start or mid-year refresh
- **Sources:** Wholesale (for cost), online (reviews and selection), and local stores (emergencies and replacement)

### Psychographics

- **Personality:** Competitive, authoritative yet approachable, energetic, analytical; strongly values personal discipline and commitment
- **Values:** Player development, respect, teamwork, punctuality, effort, sportsmanship, continual self-education
- **Interests:** Soccer tactics (study professional matches), leadership, youth mentorship, fitness and strength, equipment durability and testing
- **Lifestyle:** Balances job or career with a "second shift" of coaching in evenings and weekends; watches pro soccer; engages with local soccer community

### Customer Journey Goals by Stage

- **Pre-Purchase (AIDAR: Awareness and Interest):** Prove the store can solve his logistical nightmares (bulk ordering, on-time delivery, invoicing)
- **Transaction (AIDAR: Desire and Action):** Make spending $2,000 feel easier than spending $20
- **Post-Purchase (AIDAR: Retention):** Become the "Official Partner" for his club

## How Agents Should Use This File

When making strategic decisions, agents should anchor to specific avatars rather than generalizing.

**KIRA (Keyword Research):** When evaluating keyword priorities, ask which avatar drives the search. "Authentic Mexico jersey" leans Carlos. "Wide width youth cleats" leans Jennifer. "Best speed cleats 2026" leans Tyler. Different avatars have different conversion likelihood, AOV, and lifetime value.

**SCRIBE (On-Page SEO):** Meta descriptions and intro copy should speak to the avatar most likely to land on that page. A page targeting "soccer cleats for wide feet" should speak Jennifer's language ("Tired of returning narrow cleats? Wide-fit options for kids with growing feet"). A page targeting "Mercurial Superfly Elite" should speak Tyler's language (performance specs, pro endorsements).

**SAGE (Content Writer):** Article topics and angles should map to specific avatars. Authenticity guides for Carlos. Safety and durability guides for Jennifer. Performance comparison content for Tyler. Bulk equipment guides for Mike.

**METRIK (Reporting):** Where data allows, segment performance reporting by avatar to surface which segments are growing and which need attention.

**Master Strategist:** Use avatar context to challenge proposed priorities. If a proposal serves no clearly named avatar well, that's a signal to revisit.

The Voice of Customer quotes (especially Jennifer's) are research-backed language. Use that exact wording in copy where it fits naturally.
